Context for Leadership

How the Publishing Deal Process Works — From Interest to Signed Contract

For leadership unfamiliar with the games publishing industry, it is important to understand that a publisher expressing interest at a conference is the very beginning of a long process — not the end. The full journey from "we're interested" to a signed publishing agreement typically takes three to nine months and involves multiple sequential gates, each of which can end the process. Below is what that process looks like and where our studios currently stand.

1
Conference Interest & Follow-Up
✓ Where We Are Now
A publisher meets a studio at a conference and expresses interest. They ask the studio to send a build or submit through their portal. This is not a deal — it is an invitation to be evaluated. The vast majority of studios who receive this invitation never progress further, typically because their submission materials are not ready or not at the right standard. Post-conference interest has a shelf life of 2–4 weeks before publishers move on to their next slate. All three ShawneeXP studios are at this stage right now.
2
Evaluation Package Submission
The studio submits a full evaluation package: a playable demo build, pitch deck, development budget, market analysis, and one-page sell sheet. The publisher's internal evaluation team — producers, business development leads, market analysts — reviews everything together. This is the most critical stage for first-time studios. Publishers receive hundreds of submissions. An incomplete, inconsistent, or visually unpolished package signals inexperience and is eliminated before gameplay is even assessed. Timeline: 1–3 weeks to prepare, 2–6 weeks for publisher to review. This is what we are preparing our studios for right now.
3
Publisher Feedback & Follow-Up Meetings
If the evaluation team is interested, they schedule a follow-up call or meeting to ask deeper questions about the game, team, and business. This is where publishers test whether the founders can talk about their game commercially — not just creatively. First-time founders often struggle here because they have not been coached on how to discuss budget, scope, market positioning, and equity terms under pressure. Mentors help prepare studios for these conversations in advance. Timeline: 2–8 weeks after submission.
4
Term Sheet / Letter of Intent
A publisher who wants to move forward issues a term sheet — a non-binding summary of the proposed deal structure: funding amount, revenue split, marketing commitments, milestones, IP ownership, and platform exclusivity. This is where first-time founders most commonly make mistakes — accepting unfavorable terms out of excitement, or failing to negotiate effectively because they don't understand what is standard. Charly Harbord's role is critical at this stage. Timeline: 1–4 weeks to negotiate.
5
Due Diligence & Contract Negotiation
The publisher conducts due diligence — verifying the studio's financials, IP ownership, team contracts, and development progress. A full publishing agreement is drafted and negotiated, typically running 20–60 pages. Legal review, milestone structure, audit rights, and marketing obligations are all negotiated at this stage. First-time founders need legal and business support to avoid long-term unfavorable commitments. Timeline: 4–12 weeks.
6
Signed Deal & First Milestone Payment
The publishing agreement is signed, the studio receives its first milestone payment, and development continues under the agreed structure with milestone reviews, marketing deliverables, and publisher oversight. This is the goal — and for studios with strong evaluation packages and good mentor support, it is achievable. Total timeline from GDC interest to signed deal: typically 3–9 months.
Why Mentor Support Is Essential at This Stage
None of our studio founders have been through this process before. Without guidance, first-time studios routinely stall at Stage 2 (submission quality), stumble at Stage 3 (commercial conversations), and sign unfavorable deals at Stage 4–5. Marcus and Charly have seen this process from multiple sides — as creators, as program builders, and as industry professionals. Their involvement does not just improve the materials. It keeps the founders from making the mistakes that first-timers almost always make.
Evaluation Package Requirements

What Every Studio Needs to Submit

1
Critical
Polished Playable Demo Build
A stable, representative build of the game — typically 15–30 minutes of core gameplay. Must be bug-free, launchable without technical support, and representative of the final vision. This is the single most important element. Publishers will not make offers on a pitch deck alone.
2
Critical
Publisher-Facing Pitch Deck
A 10–15 slide deck covering: game overview and genre, core loop and unique selling points, target audience, comparable titles with commercial performance data, development status and roadmap, team credentials, and the ask (funding amount, services needed, equity offered). Must be visually polished — publishers judge professionalism immediately.
3
Critical
Development Budget & Timeline
An itemized budget covering remaining development costs through launch, broken down by discipline (programming, art, audio, QA, marketing). Paired with a milestone-based timeline showing key deliverables. This is how publishers assess financial risk — vague or inflated budgets are a common rejection trigger.
4
Important
Market Analysis & Comparable Titles
A concise analysis of the game's market position — genre size, target platforms, 3–5 comparable titles with their sales data (units sold, revenue, review counts), and a clear articulation of why this game can compete. Publishers use comps to model revenue projections. Studios that can't articulate their market signal commercial inexperience.
5
Important
Gameplay Trailer / Vertical Slice Video
A 60–90 second gameplay trailer showing the game in its best light. Does not need to be a full marketing trailer — a clean vertical slice showing core mechanics, visual style, and emotional tone is sufficient. Used both in the evaluation package and for publisher-to-publisher sharing.
6
Standard
Steam / Platform Page & Wishlist Presence
An active, well-crafted Steam page with screenshots, description copy, and a growing wishlist count. Publishers treat wishlists as a proxy for market validation — a studio with 2,000+ wishlists signals real audience demand. Page copy and capsule art must be polished and consistent with the pitch deck.
7
Standard
One-Page Executive Summary
A single-page document (often called a "sell sheet") that captures the essential pitch in under 60 seconds of reading. Used for publisher-internal forwarding when the evaluator presents to their leadership. Must include game title, genre, platform, funding ask, key comp, and a single screenshot or key art image.
